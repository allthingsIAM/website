2026 NFHS Rule Changes — All Players Must Know

These are active for the 2026 season. Ignorance of a rule does not prevent the penalty. Go over these with your team before the first practice.

Crease ban — Defense (Rules 4-18-4, 5-6-2)
No field player other than a properly equipped goalkeeper may enter their own crease with intent to block a shot. Penalty: non-releasable personal foul for illegal equipment. This applies to all defensemen.
Faceoff positioning (Rule 4-3-3e)
If a faceoff player must be repeatedly adjusted by an official, a delay-of-game penalty can be called and possession awarded to the opponent. Get your set position right in practice — not at the whistle.
Expanded holding (Rule 5-1)
Holding now includes hooking, lifting, or pinning an opponent's body with the crosse. Lift checks targeting the stick remain legal. Any crosse contact with the body in a pinning or hooking pattern is a foul.
Helmet loss (Rule 4-illegal procedure)
Losing your helmet during play and continuing is now a technical foul for illegal procedure. Fit chin straps properly before every practice and game. No exceptions.
All restarts now lateral (Rule 7-3)
All restarts after penalties now occur laterally outside the goal area. No more restarts that allow a straight sprint toward cage. Offenses and defenses need to adjust restart positioning habits in practice.
Motogrip / Motorcycle grip
Not a legal set position under NFHS rules. Any player whose top hand is rotated palm-down over the shaft (thumb pointing toward the head) will be adjusted. Repeated adjustment = delay of game. Do not drill this grip.
Injury prevention first. Any player with prior injury should clear return-to-activity with their trainer or physician before entering Phase 2. Do not skip Phase 1 to catch up.

Attack Program

Faceoff Specialists
Faceoff Program

Faceoff is the only position in lacrosse that is entirely its own skill set. The methodology here draws from the Faceoff Academy (Greg Gurenlian) and Faceoff Factory frameworks: stance first, offensive moves second, counters third, exits fourth, and live reps every session. Winning 55%+ changes games. Getting to 65%+ wins championships.

2026 NFHS Rule Update — Faceoff players: The motogrip (top hand palm-down, thumb toward head) is not a legal set position under NFHS rules. Do not drill it. Under Rule 4-3-3e, repeated adjustment by an official results in a delay-of-game penalty and possession awarded to the opponent. Get your set position right in practice so it is automatic on game day — there is no grace period at the whistle.
Phase 1 — Stance & Set Position
Master this before any move · 2026 NFHS legal · Daily
▾
The Faceoff Academy system starts here. Greg Gurenlian teaches that stance is the foundation of every offensive move. A player with a dialed-in stance gets into their move faster and controls the ball with their body — not just their hands. Every other drill in this section is useless if the stance isn't right.

Legal Set Position — Coach Check List

Foot Position
Feet slightly wider than shoulder width. Weight forward on the balls of the feet — not on the heels. Hips low. Front knee bent ~90°. You should be able to explode laterally or forward from this position without a loading step.
Foundation
Hand Position (Legal)
Top hand: palm faces away, thumb up or neutral. Never palm-down — that's the motogrip and is illegal at NFHS level. Bottom hand: standard overhand grip, 8–10 inches below top hand. Both hands on the shaft only. Stick flat on the ground, head even with the center line.
Rules complianceChecked every rep
Body Angle
Torso leans forward, over the stick. Head up, eyes on the ball and your opponent's hands simultaneously. Shoulders square to the midfield line — not angled. Weight is loaded toward the ball side.
Leverage
Stance Hold Drill
Get into perfect set position. Hold for 10 seconds. Coach adjusts any fault: heel weight, raised hips, collapsed top hand, stick off the ground. 10 reps. The goal is a position that feels identical every single time — muscle memory before movement.
10 holds × 10sDaily

Phase 2 — The Three Offensive Moves
FOA System: Quick Clamp · Power Clamp · Plunger · Master one before adding the next
▾
Gurenlian's framework: There are three offensive faceoff movements — Quick Clamp, Power Clamp, and Plunger. The right-hand movement is the same on all three: drive the ball stop directly into the ball. What changes is the angle, timing, and exit. A player who owns all three keeps defenders guessing. A player who only owns one gets scouted and stopped.

The Three Moves

Quick Clamp
Pure hand speed, no body commitment. Drive the ball stop down onto the ball the instant the whistle fires. Wrists snap fast and hard. Body follows — don't lead with your body or you telegraph the move. This is your primary weapon and your fastest option. Reps: get to where the motion happens before you consciously think.
30 solo reps3 setsPrimary weapon
Power Clamp
Same clamp action but with full hip and body engagement. You commit your weight forward and through the ball. Slower to initiate than the quick clamp, but far harder to counter once locked. Used against bigger or faster opponents to win with leverage instead of reaction time. Hips drive — not just wrists.
20 repsLeverage dominant
Plunger
Instead of clamping across the ball, drive the stick head straight down on top of the opponent's stick with force — a spring-like plunging action that pops the ball loose forward. Highly effective against players who go low and flat with their clamp. The ball pops out in front; you're already moving to it. Requires strong wrists and a flexible head.
20 repsAdvancedPop move

Solo Drilling Protocol (FOA Method)

Quick Chops — 10 Seconds
In set position over the ball. Go back and forth over the ball as fast as possible without touching it for 10 seconds. This is a pure hand-speed warm-up. Count contacts per 10 seconds; track and try to beat weekly.
6 × 10s30s restHand speed
Horizontal Rapid Fire
Set up 8–10 balls across the midfield line with just enough space between them to get set. Start at one end. On the alarm or whistle: clamp the first ball, toss it out forward or backward, get to the next ball, repeat. Full speed down the line. Builds transition speed and move automation.
3 runs per directionConditioning
Fast Hands Reflex
Place a ball 3 inches from your bare hand. Set alarm for every 6 seconds. When it goes off, grab the ball as fast as possible — no stick. Trains the reflex pattern that firing your clamp relies on. Simple, requires no equipment, can be done anywhere.
20 grabsReflex
Resisted Clamp (Band)
Anchor a light resistance band to a fence. Loop around the throat of the stick below the head. Get in set position and clamp hard against the tension. Focus on a violent snap motion — same muscles, more resistance. After clamp, work exits against the band tension from different angles. Builds clamp power and exit strength simultaneously.
4 sets10 repsStrength

Phase 3 — Counters & Defensive Moves
Only add after offensive moves are automatic · Jam · Rake · Counter Clamp
▾
A predictable FOGO gets scouted by the third faceoff. Counters are what separate a high school player who wins sometimes from one who wins consistently. Per the FOA approach: go into every faceoff assuming you might lose the initial move. Counters trained at game speed turn a clean loss into a 50/50.

Counter Moves

Rake
When the opponent wins the clamp, don't fight it — pull your head across and drag the ball out sideways toward your body. Low wrists, quick lateral pull, immediate body shift to protect. Your rake beats their clamp when executed clean. Drill it as a counter specifically: let your partner win the clamp every rep, then rake.
20 repsCounter to clamp
The Jam
Drive your stick head directly into your opponent's stick as they try to pull the ball out — stopping their exit cold. Forces a contested ball. Used when you know you're slower to the clamp. Keep center of gravity low, use body weight into the jam. A well-executed jam beats a fast sloppy clamp win every time.
20 repsDisrupt exit
Jam + Counter Clamp
You jam their clamp attempt, then immediately re-clamp over the top of both sticks. The timing window is narrow — the jam must land at the same moment they try to pull. Body and hip drive determines who wins. No illegal grip change required.
15 repsAdvanced
Shovel Counter
When the ball pops loose between both sticks, shovel it sideways or backward to your wing before either player can fully control it. Uses a quick wrist scoop motion, one hand if needed. Best executed when you read a contested or popped ball early — don't wait to see where it goes.
15 repsWing play
Read + React Drill
Partner intentionally wins the clamp every rep. Your job: read their exit direction and fire the correct counter — rake left, rake right, jam, or shovel. Partner calls the move silently with hand signal after the whistle; you must react to what you see, not what you expected. Trains anticipation, not just mechanics.
20 repsGame realism
Opponent Tendencies Scouting
Before every game, coach provides a short report: does their FOGO clamp quick or power? Rake left or right? Push through or go to wing? Player adjusts their primary counter accordingly. This is not optional — FOA teaches that reading your opponent's body language and set position before the whistle is as important as the move itself.

Phase 4 — Exits & Wing Coordination
Win the draw, then keep possession · FOGO + wings drill together
▾
Winning the faceoff is only half the job. Per Air Force coach Will Corrigan and FOA methodology: wings must know all three basic moves (clamp, rake, shovel) and their positioning responsibilities for each one. FOGO and wings must be on the same page before every draw. A FOGO who wins 65% but only converts 70% of those into possession is no different from one winning 45%.

Exit Drills — FOGO Solo

Clamp + Wing Outlet
Clamp the ball, secure with body, immediately throw one-hand outlet to the near wing. Wing player must be moving — they do not stand still and wait. Outlet lands ahead of the wing in stride. 10 reps each side. Time from whistle to wing catch: target under 1.5 seconds.
10 reps each sidePrimary exit
Clamp + Push Through
After clamp, instead of going to wing — push ball through X and burst forward yourself. Requires full hip drive. The FOGO becomes a ball-carrier in transition. Best when both wing defenders collapse on the wings. Drill it specifically against a scenario where both wings are covered.
10 repsRun-it-yourself
Rake + Back Wing Outlet
After a rake, ball is usually behind the FOGO. Body turn is critical — pivot over the ball, shield with your back, throw back-wing outlet or carry through. Drill the pivot specifically: slow it down until the footwork is clean, then build to speed.
10 repsRake exit
Scoop-Turn-Clear
Ball at feet after a contested draw. Scoop, pivot 180°, outlet immediately. No stopping — one flowing motion. The 50/50 scoop into a clean clear is a game-changing play. Drill it until it's as automatic as any faceoff move.
10 reps each side

Wing Alignment by Move

Clamp → Wing Left
Left wing is the primary outlet. Right wing holds their lane as a secondary option and crash lane if left wing is covered. Wings do not run until the ball is clamped — premature movement telegraphs the exit and allows the opposing wing to cut the outlet off.
Team drill
Rake → Back Wing
After a rake, ball typically goes back toward the defensive side. The back wing must crash to the trail ball immediately. The front wing drops into a defensive support position. This swap happens on instinct — drill it so both wings react to the rake automatically.
Team drill
Shovel → Trail Ball
After a shovel, ball goes sideways. Both wings crash the ball hard. First wing there scoops and immediately looks upfield. Second wing provides an immediate outlet option. Speed to the ball wins this scenario — not technique.
Team drill

Phase 5 — Reaction Training & Live Reps
Randomized cadence · Tournament mode · Track win % weekly
▾
FOA's draw day structure — used in their weekly training — runs players through live reps with randomized cadences and tournament mode for in-game pressure. The goal is not just to get reps but to harden the player mentally. A player who only practices with predictable whistles will freeze or false-start in games.

Reaction Protocols

Randomized Cadence Whistle
Coach blows whistle with no predictable timing — vary the pause between "set" and the whistle from 0.5 to 4 seconds. Player cannot anticipate. Must react only to the sound. Call the move before set: "Quick clamp left." Track false starts separately from missed whistle. Goal: zero false starts by Phase 3.
30 repsRandomizedReaction
Visual Ref Read
Coach simulates ref arm motion — the wrist drop before the whistle is the real cue elite FOGOs read. Player reacts to the visual cue. Alternated with audio-only reps so the player trains both separately. At game speed, the visual cue fires 0.1–0.2 seconds before the sound reaches the player.
20 repsAdvanced reaction
Live 1v1 — Full Sequence
Full faceoff: set, whistle, move, counter, ground ball, outlet. Loser rides. One player wins possession and they clear it. This is the unit — not just the draw. The entire sequence must be trained together. Minimum 20 live reps per session.
20+ repsGame speed
Tournament Mode
Run a bracket: 4 FOGOs compete, track wins, losers are eliminated. Winner gets 2 minutes of rest, then faces the next challenger. Simulates the game pressure of consecutive faceoffs against different opponents. Run this at the end of every Wednesday stick session.
15 min blockPressure
Win % Tracking
Record every live rep result. Calculate weekly win percentage. Post it. Phase 1 target: 50%+. Phase 2 target: 55%+. Phase 3 target: 60%+. Phase 4 target: maintain 60%+ while adjusting to opponent counters. This is not optional data — it's how you measure development.
Metrics
Film Review Session
Once per week (Wednesday), review recorded reps from the previous session. Check set position, hand legality, timing of move, exit decision. FOA records and shares video with players — do the same with your phone if you don't have dedicated film. 10 minutes of self-review per week accelerates development faster than 30 extra reps.

Post-Draw Ground Ball Sequence
Win the draw, then win the 50/50 — both must be trained
▾

Good GB Warm-Up — Forward

Strength Program — Faceoff Specialists
3 days/week · Grip · Wrist torque · Hip explosion

Faceoff strength is specific. Grip, forearm, and wrist strength determine clamp power and exit control. Hip strength and explosion determine ball protection and post-draw movement. This program is purpose-built for the position — not a general lifting template.

Mobility Finisher — End of Every Session (10 min)

  • 90-90 Hip Stretch — 90 seconds each side
  • Couch Stretch — 60 seconds each side. Hip flexor mobility for sprinting.
  • T-Spine Rotation on Foam Roller — 10 reps each way. Opens the throwing plane.
  • Shoulder Sleeper Stretch — 60 seconds each side. Prevents posterior capsule tightness from throwing volume.
  • Ankle Circle + Banded Distraction — 10 circles each ankle. Often the limiting factor in squat depth and cutting mechanics.
For younger JV players (under 15): Prioritize movement quality over load. Use bodyweight or light dumbbells for Phases 1 and 2. Never load a bad movement pattern. One extra set of mobility work replaces the last heavy set in every exercise.
Recovery & Nutrition Guidelines
Non-negotiable habits
▾

Sleep

8–9 hours per night for athletes in active training. Two consecutive nights under 7 hours reduces sprint performance by 5–8%. No training adaptation compensates for chronic sleep debt.

Nutrition Basics

  • Protein: 0.7–1.0g per pound of bodyweight daily. Spread across 3–4 meals. Chicken, eggs, Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, fish.
  • Carbohydrates: Train days need them. Rice, oats, potatoes, fruit. Don't fear carbs on training days.
  • Hydration: Minimum half bodyweight in ounces of water daily. Add 16oz per hour of outdoor training in heat.
  • Pre-workout: Eat 90 min before. Carbs + protein. Not a protein shake alone.
  • Post-workout: Within 45 minutes — protein + carbs. Chocolate milk is actually fine and cheap.

Active Recovery (Sunday)

  • 20-minute walk or easy bike ride
  • Full foam roll sequence: quads, IT band, thoracic spine, lats, calves
  • 10-minute stretch: hip flexors, hamstrings, chest, shoulders
  • No organized training. The body adapts on rest days.
